## The sweet spot

I've always found myself in the middle of things. Even in life, when I think back to my cultural identity crisis growing up, not Yoruba enough to the Yorubas, not Hausa enough to the Hausas, I've operated in the in-between. And in my career it's the same story: not too PMy, not too devy. Sitting at the intersection of product, engineering, and data. What I've come to realize is that I actually excel in both directions, and my performance speaks to that.

There's never really been a role title that cleanly captures this. But I've been fortunate that even though my role is officially PM, I've been allowed to operate at that sweet spot anyway. Writing my own KQL. Prototyping data models. Building MCP plugins for my org's Copilot CLI. Jumping into codebases when there's a fire. That's what keeps me sane. Shout out to the managers, devs, and partners who have never made me feel like I was coloring outside the lines. People genuinely didn't have a problem with it, and I'm fortunate.

Now, though, it feels like there's actually a role emerging that names this thing. **Forward Deployed Engineer.**

## What is an FDE, really?

Palantir invented the modern version. In their model, an FDE gets dropped into a customer's problem, a bank, an oil major, a hospital system, and builds the solution end-to-end. Talk to users. Frame the problem. Model the data. Write the code. Ship the product. Rinse and repeat with the next customer.

The AI wave is copying this pattern hard. OpenAI, Anthropic, Scale, Cursor, Ramp, Sierra, Decagon, Harvey, Perplexity, they all have some version of an FDE, Applied AI Engineer, or Deployed Engineer role. The job is basically: sit inside a customer's real problem, prototype the AI solution, and hand back either a shipped product or a repeatable pattern the core product team can generalize.

So an FDE is a PM, a SWE, an ML engineer, and a solutions architect collapsed into one seat, with a bias toward shipping the ugly thing that works this week over the pretty thing that ships next quarter.

If you're a PM reading this and squinting because that sounds like the interesting parts of your job minus the meetings and the roadmap politics... yeah. Same.

## PM vs FDE, the honest version

The comparison I keep running in my head is less about which role is "better" and more about where each one is *heading.*

**PM at a big co pays you to influence.** Your leverage is the number of engineers, designers, and partner teams you can align around a bet. The ladder rewards judgment, communication, and political durability. It scales you into GPM, Director, VP, CPO. The comp is predictable and the ceiling is high, but the medium is people, not product. When the wave of a new technology hits, you route around it with roadmaps and OKRs. You don't touch the thing.

**FDE at an AI-native company pays you to ship.** Your leverage is how fast you can turn a real customer problem into a working system that solves it. The ladder rewards taste, technical range, and speed. It scales you into Staff FDE, Head of Applied AI, or founder. The downside is that if the company you're at can't productize the patterns you're finding, you can get stuck doing bespoke customer work forever.

The two roles used to sit closer together. A senior PM at a good product co and a senior FDE at a good applied co were doing recognizably similar work: talk to users, frame the problem, drive the solution. What's changed is that LLMs eat a huge slice of the traditional PM workflow. The PRD, the first-pass data cut, the competitive analysis, the polished deck, all of it is 5x faster with an LLM in the loop. The marginal PM is now cheaper. The PMs who compound from here are the ones who can build alongside their thinking, because "having good ideas" is no longer scarce.

That's the whole thesis. **People are arguing that PM is compressing, is it? FDE is where the intersection I've always lived in is finally getting priced correctly, is it?**

## What I've been doing anyway

The interesting thing is that whether or not the FDE role ever formally shows up on my resume, most of what would prepare someone for it is stuff I've been doing at my day job for a while. Not as prep. Just because it's how I like to work.

- **Owning real technical artifacts.** For me that's the MCP-based Copilot CLI plugins I built for my org and the KQL-heavy data work I do. Code with my name on it, sitting next to a PM title.
- **Going deep in one modern AI stack end-to-end.** Plugin building, MCP tooling, natural-language-to-data flows. Not another to-do app. Something that touches real data and has real users.
- **Keeping the engineering fundamentals warm.** I still read code more than most PMs I know. Still jump into codebases when it's faster than filing a ticket.
- **Portfolio over pedigree.** A website, a GitHub, things people can click. Feels obvious but most PMs don't have one.

None of this is a departure playbook. It's just what "operating at the sweet spot" looks like from the inside. If the FDE-shaped role ever fits me formally, great. If it doesn't, I'm still a sharper PM for having done it.

## And maybe your own company

FDE-shaped roles don't only live at AI-native startups anymore. Big cos are quietly hiring for versions of this: "Applied Scientist," "Solutions Architect," "Deployment Engineer," "Customer Zero Engineer." Some of them are internal. That's actually the part I'm most curious about, where the FDE shape fits inside a big co that's already building AI end-to-end. The best cases I've seen for people finding those seats start with them quietly demonstrating the work before asking for the title.

## Where I'm landing

I'm not announcing anything. I'm still a PM at Microsoft on Consumer Commerce, and I love the work. But the frame I'm carrying into the next couple of years is: **the intersection of product and engineering has always been where I do my best work.** For a long time that intersection didn't have a job title. Now it does, and it's called Forward Deployed Engineer.

Whether that shape ever fits me formally, externally or internally or not at all, the practice is the same: keep building things with my own hands, keep sharpening the engineering muscles, keep writing about it out loud. Same work I've been doing. Just with a name for it now.
