I was digging through an old folder this week and found an essay I wrote in September 2020, right at the start of my sophomore year at Fisk. The prompt was some version of "what's your biggest challenge?" and 18-year-old me had a lot to say.

Here's a slice of it:

> Not many would want to take a 22-hour flight, but the thirst for quality education has made me among the few who are willing to take that risk. It could only take determination to not only transition from high school to college but also make the transition by crossing national borders... In Nigeria, a person that is asked where he comes from is most likely to mention his ethnic group than anything, but here (in the USA) the American indicates his state of origin. Fortunately, I have also LEARNED that people have different views and backgrounds, and the ability to tolerate these views (despite having an opinion on what should be normal) is essential for survival in a diverse community.

Reading that back six years later, two things stand out.

## What 18-year-old me was actually saying

The essay is dressed up as being about "transitioning from high school to college," but that's not really what it's about. It's about **assumptions breaking down**. Every paragraph is some version of "I thought X, but it turned out Y." I thought college would be less work. I thought "state" was just an administrative label. I thought tolerance was a nice-to-have, not the load-bearing wall of a diverse community.

At 18, my biggest challenge was that I had a model of how the world worked, and reality was busy handing me counter-examples faster than I could update the model. Every day was a small identity crisis wearing the costume of an ordinary Tuesday.

I'm also a little charmed by how earnest it is. The all-caps `LEARNED`. The rhetorical question about ethnic groups. The way I ended it with a moral instead of just letting the story sit. It reads like someone who had just landed in Nashville with two suitcases (just had to add two suitcases for the plot lol) with his uncle and a lot to prove, which... yeah, that was me.

## What I'd say now

If you asked me the same question today, in July 2026, sitting in Redmond after four years of shipping commerce products at Xbox, the answer is not "adjusting to a new country." That challenge got quietly solved somewhere between my first Fisk winter and my third Microsoft standup.

The challenge now is subtler and, honestly, harder to name. It's something like: **how do you keep the compass steady when the map keeps getting bigger?**

At 18, my ambition had one axis: get to the US, do the thing, make it worth the 22-hour flight. Every decision was measured against a very short list. Now the list is longer. There's work I care about (helping build the commerce systems that hundreds of millions of gamers touch). There's a version of myself I'm trying to be as a Nigerian in America, as a first-gen professional, as someone whose parents can't Google what I do for a living. There's the question of what I owe to the communities that got me here and how to actually pay it back instead of just posting about it. And there's the quieter question, the one nobody asks in interviews: **what am I optimizing for, really?**

18-year-old me would have written a tidy paragraph about "finding balance." 24-year-old me knows that balance is a lie invented by people who want to sell you a planner. The real thing is more like... calibration. Constantly checking whether the thing you're pointed at is still the thing you'd choose if you were choosing fresh today.

## The part I got right

Rereading the essay, I want to update almost every claim except one:

> We, usually, have assumptions about the unfamiliar environments which turn out false once we get accustomed to the environment.

Still true. Still the whole game. The unfamiliar environments have changed. Fisk became Microsoft. Microsoft became a specific team, then a specific problem space, then a specific set of judgment calls about what to build next. Each one felt strange, then felt normal, then handed me a new set of assumptions to unlearn. That loop hasn't stopped and I don't think it's supposed to.

If 18-year-old me is reading this from somewhere in a Fisk dorm in September 2020: you're going to be fine. The 22-hour flight was the easy part. Keep the essay. It'll come in handy in six years.

tudum.
